### Building a Secure Organization in the Cyber World

![](/images/1_sWahjiY8Mezr6QWu9JJDAg.png)

It seems logical that every business, whether commercial or non-profit, understands that creating a secure organization is important for long-term success. When a business implements and maintains a strong security posture, it can enjoy numerous benefits. A secure organization can use its security program as a marketing tool and show customers how much it values ​​their business and how it stands to protect their information. But most importantly, a secure organization will not have to spend time and money detecting security breaches and responding to the consequences of those breaches.

![](/images/1_m9xT3lhsreu1zC6hSYK13Q.png)

Information Security

### A. Obstacles to Security

Let's take a closer look at the obstacles that make it difficult to create a secure organization.

Security is inherently demanding, and the more robust the security mechanisms, the more demanding the process becomes. Employees in an organization have a job to do; They want to get to work immediately. From passwords to multi-factor authentication, many security mechanisms are viewed as impediments to productivity. One of the current trends in security is adding whole disk encryption to laptops. Although this is a highly recommended security process, it adds a second login step before a computer user can actually get to work. Even though this step only adds a minute to the login process, over the course of a year that adds up to four hours of lost productivity.

We only have to watch the Transportation Security Administration (TSA) security queues at any airport to fully understand the frustration caused by security measures. Just watch the frustration as a particular item is passed through the scanner for the third time while you're running late to board a passenger plane. Security practices are based on a sliding scale; One end of the scale is complete security and complete inconvenience, the other end is complete distrust and complete ease of use. When we implement any security mechanism, it must be placed on the scale where the level of security and ease of use matches the level of risk acceptable to the organization.

#### Powerful and Complex Computers

Home computers have become repositories of personal data. Our computers now contain wedding videos, scanned family photos, music libraries, movie collections, and financial and health information. Because computers contain so much familiar information and are so integrated into life, we have forgotten how powerful and complex devices they are. Not long ago, computers as powerful as our desktops and laptops would fill one or more large rooms. In addition, today's computers present a “user-friendly” face to the world.

![](/images/1_Leuvd-FleRoFfKzpN26UhA.jpeg)

ENIAC Computer

Most people don't know how computers actually work and what goes on behind the scenes. Things like the Windows Registry, ports, and services are completely unknown to most users and poorly understood by many computer professionals. For example, many people still believe that the Windows login password protects data on the computer. Rather, someone can remove the hard drive from the computer, plug it into another computer as a drive, or place it in a USB drive enclosure, and all data can be easily accessed.

#### Uninformed Computer Users

Many computer users believe they “know everything about computers” because they are skilled at creating spreadsheets (like Excel), word processing documents (like Word), and presentations (like Powerpoint), in short, office programs.

These “Savvy Users” have advanced beyond the application basics, but many still do not even understand basic security concepts. Many users will haphazardly install software and visit questionable Web sites despite the fact that these actions may violate company policies. “Bad guys,” people who want to steal information from or damage computer systems, have also identified the end user as a weak link in the security chain.ir.

![](/images/1_EVJHO-mSs1IsrPDez7zynQ.jpeg)

End User Risk

As companies began to invest more money in perimeter defenses, attackers began to look for the path of least resistance. They send malicious software as an email attachment and ask recipients to open the attachment. Despite being told not to open attachments from unknown senders or simply not to open attachments at all, employees continually violate this policy, wreaking havoc on their networks.

![](/images/1_5sBt5Lq39KhCLqrEy3goxQ.jpeg)

Phishing

Phishing scams have been very effective at convincing individuals to provide their personal online banking and credit card information. Why would an attacker bother to breach an organization's defenses when end users are more than willing to hand over the keys to their bank accounts? Addressing the threat posed by untrained and careless end users is an important part of any security program.

#### Computers Built Without Security in Mind

During the development of personal computers (PCs), no thought was given to security. The first PCs were very simple devices with limited processing power, no keyboard, and were programmed by turning a series of knobs. They were developed almost as a curiosity. Even as they became more advanced and complex, all efforts were focused on developing greater sophistication and capabilities; no one thought there would be security issues. We only have to look at some of the first computers, such as the Berkeley Enterprises Geniac, Heathkit EC-1 or MITS Altair 8800, to understand why security was not an issue at the time. The development of computers focused on what they could do, not how they could be attacked!

![](/images/1_Bf4of7Vzp-zoPg86RGgYAQ.jpeg)

When computers began to be connected to each other, the main goal was not to protect information, but to provide the ability to share information. The Internet was originally designed for military applications, but eventually moved to colleges and universities where its core principle was information sharing.

#### The Current Trend is to Share, Not to Protect

Even now, despite stories about compromised data, people still want to share their data with everyone. Web-based applications make this easier than attaching a file to an email. Social networking platforms like Meta provide the ability to share material: "Send messages, files, links and events to your friends. Create a network of friends and share things. It's free and easy."

In addition, many online storage sites such as DropSend and FilesAnywhere provide file sharing. These sites can bypass security mechanisms, allowing private data to leave an organization.

#### Data Accessible from Anywhere

As if employees' desire to share data wasn't enough of a threat to private information, many business professionals want to access data from a variety of devices wherever they work. To be productive, employees now demand access to data and contact information on their laptops, desktops, home computers and mobile devices. Therefore, information technology (IT) departments must now provide the ability to synchronize data across multiple devices. If the IT department is unable or unwilling to provide this capability, employees have the power to take matters into their own hands.

The previously mentioned online storage sites can be accessed both from home and office or wherever there is an Internet connection. Although it is possible to block access to some of these sites, it is not possible to block access to all of them. Some may seem quite harmless. For many people, Google's free email service, Gmail, is a great tool that offers a very solid service for free. Few people are aware that Gmail provides storage that can be used to store files, not just emails. The Gspace add-on for the Firefox browser provides an FTP-like interface within Firefox that allows users to transfer files from the computer to their Gmail account. The ease with which data can be transferred outside of a company's control makes it much more difficult to secure an organization's data.

#### Security Is Not About Hardware and Software

Many businesses believe that they can create a secure infrastructure if they purchase enough equipment.believes. Firewalls, intrusion detection systems, antivirus programs, and two-factor authentication products are just a few of the tools that can help protect a network and its data. It is important to keep in mind that no single product or combination of products will create a secure institution. Security is a process; There is no tool that you can “set and forget”. All security products are only as secure as the people who configure and maintain them. Purchasing and implementing security products should be only a percentage of the security budget. Employees tasked with maintaining safety devices must be provided with sufficient time, training and equipment to properly support the products. Unfortunately, in many organizations, security activities lag behind support activities. Highly skilled security professionals are often tasked with help desk projects such as resetting forgotten passwords, repairing stuck printers, and setting up new employee workstations.

#### Bad Guys Are Very Sophisticated

Once upon a time, the hacker was often portrayed as a lonely teenager with poor social skills who broke into systems for nothing more than bragging rights. But as e-commerce has evolved, the profile of hackers has also changed.

Because there are now large collections of credit card numbers and intellectual property, organized hacker groups have formed to operate as businesses. A document published in 2008 makes this clear: Cybercrime companies that operate like real-world companies have begun to emerge and are growing larger thanks to the profits they make. Forget about individual hackers or groups of hackers with common goals. What you and your company need to worry about are hierarchical cybercriminal organizations where each cybercriminal has their own role and reward system.

Nowadays, when institutions are attacked by groups of highly motivated and skilled hackers, it has become necessary to create a secure infrastructure.

#### Management Sees Security as a Burden on Profitability

For most organizations, the cost of establishing a strong security posture is viewed as a necessary evil, similar to purchasing insurance. Organizations don't want to spend money on this, but the risks of not making the purchase outweigh the costs. Because of this attitude, it is extremely difficult to create a secure organization. This attitude stems from the fact that requests for security tools are often backed by documentation that shows the average cost of a security incident rather than demonstrating the more tangible benefits of a strong security posture.

![](/images/1_xgVvQ2QkFScPSanG1cBoRw.png)

The fact that IT experts speak a different language than management further deepens the problem. IT professionals often focus on technology, period. Management, on the other hand, focuses on revenue. Concepts such as profitability, asset depreciation, return on investment, realization and total cost of ownership are the basic foundations of management. These are foreign concepts to most IT professionals.

Realistically, although it is helpful for management to take steps to learn some of the basics of information technology, IT professionals should take the initiative and learn some basic business concepts. Learning these concepts is beneficial for the organization because the technical background can be implemented in a cost-effective manner and is beneficial for career development for IT professionals.

A Google search for “business skills for IT professionals” will bring up a number of training programs that may be useful. For those who do not have the time or inclination to attend a class, some very useful materials can be found online.

Whichever approach is taken, it is important to remember that any tangible cost savings or revenue generation should be leveraged when calling for new security products, tools or policies. Security experts often overlook the value of keeping Web portals open for employees. A database used by a salesperson to enter contracts or purchases or to control inventory will help generate more revenue if there is no downtime. An inaccessible or hacked database is useless for generating revenue.

Strong security to gain competitive advantage in the marketavailable. Having secure systems that are accessible 24 hours a day, seven days a week means an organization can reach and communicate with its customers and prospects more efficiently. An organization that is recognized as a good guardian of customer records and information may make its security record part of its brand. This is no different than a car company being known for its safety record. For example, in discussions of cars and safety, Volvo is always the first manufacturer mentioned.

### B. Building a Secure Organization in Ten Steps

Having identified some of the challenges to creating a secure organization, let's now examine 10 ways to successfully create a secure organization. The following steps will put a business in a solid security posture.

#### 1. Assess Risks and Threats

Where should you start when trying to create a secure organization? One common belief is that you must first identify your assets and allocate security resources based on the value of each asset. While this approach is effective, it can lead to some significant security vulnerabilities. For example, an infrastructure asset may not have a high value, but it should be protected with the same effort as a high-value asset. Otherwise, it could become an entry point into your network and gain access to valuable data.

Another approach is to start by assessing the threats to your organization and data.

The first place to start is to identify risks based on an organization's infrastructure model. What is the infrastructure required to support the operational needs of the business? A small business operating out of a single office has less risk than an organization that operates across multiple sites, has a mobile workforce that uses a variety of handheld devices, and offers products or services through a Web-based interface. An organization with a large number of remote workers should take steps to protect their private information, which could potentially reside on personal computers outside company control. An organization with widely dispersed and disparate systems will have greater risk potential than a centralized organization using uniform systems.

Are there any specific threats to your business? Have senior managers been accused of improper activities that would encourage shareholders or employees to attack the business? Are there people with a vendetta against the company over real or imagined insults or accidents? Does the community have a history of hostility towards the organization? A risk management or security team should ask these questions regularly to assess risks in real time. This part of the security process is often overlooked due to the focus on the daily workload.

Businesses in certain sectors are targeted more frequently and more devotedly than those in other sectors. Financial institutions and online retailers are targeted because “that's where the money is.” Drug manufacturers can be targeted for stealing intellectual property, but they can also be targeted by special interest groups, such as those who do not believe in testing drugs on live animals.

**Global Threats**Businesses often focus so narrowly on their local sphere of influence that they forget that once they have a network connected to the Internet, they are now connected to the rest of the world. If malware detected on the other side of the world targets the same software used in your organization, you can be sure that you will eventually be affected by this malware. Additionally, if extremist groups in other countries are targeting your industry, you will be targeted too.

Once threats and risks are identified, you can take one of four steps:

* **Ignore the risk**. This is never an acceptable answer. It's simply burying your head in the sand and hoping the problem goes away — the business world of not wearing a helmet while riding a motorcycleequivalent in
* **Accept the risk**. When the cost of eliminating the risk is greater than the risk itself, an organization will often decide to accept the risk. This is a viable option as long as the organization takes the necessary time to assess the risk.
* **Transferring the risk**. Organizations with limited staff or other resources may decide to transfer risk. One method of transferring risk is to purchase specialized insurance that targets a specific risk.
* **Mitigation of risk**. Most organizations mitigate risk by using appropriate resources to minimize risks to their networks.

For organizations looking to identify and measure risks to their networks and information assets, CERT offers a suite of free tools to assist with the project.

Another risk assessment tool that can be helpful is the **Risk Management Framework** (RMF) tool.

#### 2- Beware of Common Misconceptions

When addressing an organization's security needs, it's common for professionals to fall prey to some very common misconceptions. Perhaps the most common misconception is that business is vague, uncomplicated or boring, meaning it is not a target for malicious activity. Businesses must understand that every network connected to the internet is a potential target, regardless of the type of business.

Attackers try to gain access to a network and its systems for various reasons. The first is to look around to see what they can find. Regardless of the type of business, employee information will most likely be stored in one of the systems. This includes Social Security numbers and other personal information. This type of information is always a goal.

Another possibility is that the attacker changes the information he finds or reconfigures systems to behave abnormally. These types of attackers are not interested in financial gain; It's just the tech version of teenagers soaping windows, laying eggs on cars, and covering properties with toilet paper. He attacks because he finds it fun to do so. These attackers may also use systems to store child pornography or stolen data such as credit card numbers. If a system is not secure, attackers can store such materials on your system and access them at their leisure.

The last possibility is that an attacker uses the hacked systems to attack other unprotected networks and systems. Computers can be used to launch denial-of-service (DoS) attacks, transmit spam, or spread malware. Simply put, no computer or network is immune to attack.

Another common misconception is that an organization is immune from problems caused by its employees, i.e. “We rely on all our employees, so we don't need to focus our energy on protecting our assets from them.” While this is common in small businesses where owners know everyone, it also occurs in larger organizations where companies believe they only hire “professionals.” It's important to remember that no matter how well job candidates present themselves, a business can never know everything about an employee's background. Therefore, it is important for businesses to conduct pre-employment background checks for all employees. It is also important that these background checks are performed properly and completely.

Another common misconception relates to information technology professionals. Many companies pay their IT staff very high salaries because they understand that having a properly functioning technical infrastructure is important to the ongoing success of the company. Since staff are skilled at setting up and maintaining systems and networks, it is a general assumption that they know everything there is to know about computers.  
While an individual may be very knowledgeable and technologically advanced, it is important to recognize that no one knows everything about computers. Because management does not understand the technology, it is not in a very good position to evaluate a person's depth of knowledge and experience in the field. Decisions are often based on certifications a person has achieved throughout their career. Although certifications can be used to determine a person's level of proficiency, a lot of emphasis is placed on them. Many certifications require nothing more than some time and dedication to study for and pass a certification exam. Some training companies also guarantee that a person will pass the certification exam.It also offers training camps. It is possible for people to obtain certification without real-world experience with the operating systems, applications, or hardware addressed by the certification. When evaluating a person's competency, look first at their experience level and background, and if the person has achieved certifications as well as having significant real-world experience, the certification is likely a reflection of the employee's true abilities.

#### 3- Provide Security Training to IT Staff — Now and Always

Just as implementing a robust and secure environment is a dynamic process, creating a highly qualified staff of security experts is also a dynamic process. It's important to keep in mind that even though an organization's technical infrastructure doesn't change very often, new vulnerabilities are discovered and new attacks are launched on a regular basis. In addition, few organizations have a static infrastructure; Employees are constantly requesting new software and more technology is being added to increase efficiency. Each new addition likely adds additional vulnerabilities.

It is important that IT staff are prepared to detect and respond to new threats and vulnerabilities. Those who want to gain a deep understanding of security are advised to start with a vendor-neutral program. A vendor-neutral program is one that focuses on concepts rather than specific products. The SANS (SysAdmin, Audit, Network, Security) Institute offers two introductory programs: Introduction to Information Security (Security 301) , a five-day class designed for people just getting started in the security field, and SANS Security Essentials Bootcamp (Security 401) , a six-day class designed for people with some security experience. Each class is also available as a self-study program, and each can be used to prepare for a specific certification.

Another option is to start with a program that follows CompTia Security certification requirements, such as Global Knowledge Essentials of Information Security. Some universities offer similar programs.

Once a person has a good basic background in security, he or she must undergo vendor-specific training to apply the concepts learned to specific applications and security devices.

Arguably one of the best ways to determine whether an employee has a strong grasp of information security concepts is to see if they hold the Certified Information Systems Security Professional (CISSP) certification. For this certification, candidates are tested on their understanding of the following 10 knowledge areas:

*Access control
* Application security
* Business continuity and disaster recovery planning
* Cryptography
* Information security and risk management
* Legal, regulations, compliance, and investigations
* Operations security
* Physical (environmental) security
* Security architecture and design
* Telecommunications and network security

What makes this certification so valuable is that the candidate must have at least five years of professional experience in information security or four years of experience and a college degree. To maintain certification, a certified individual must participate in 120 hours of continuing professional education during the three-year certification cycle. This ensures that CISSP credential holders stay up to date on current trends in security. Maintained by CISSP certification (ISC)2.

#### 4- Think “Outside the Box”

For most businesses, the threat to their intellectual assets and technical infrastructure comes from “bad guys” sitting outside their organization and trying to get in. These organizations essentially “surround” their assets by establishing strong perimeter defenses. However, in-house employees may access private information to do their jobs and often disseminate this information to areas that are no longer under the employer's control. This dissemination of data is generally not done with malicious intent, but simply to enable employees to access the data so they can perform their job responsibilities more efficiently. This also becomes an issue when an employee leaves a job (or a current employee loses something, such as a laptop on which private information was stored) and the organization takes no steps to collect or control private information held by the former employee.

One of the most overlooked threats to intellectual property isUSB Flash drives are now ubiquitous. It is a very convenient way to transfer data between computers.

These devices are not the only threat to data. Since other devices can also be connected to the computer via the USB port, digital cameras, MP3 players and external hard drives can now be used to remove data from a computer and the network to which it is connected. Most people accept that external hard drives pose a threat, but they do not consider other devices to be a threat.

Fortunately, Microsoft Windows keeps track of devices connected to a system in a Registry key called HKEY\_Local\_Machine\System\ControlSet00x\Enum\USBStor. It might be interesting to look at this switch on your own computer to see what kind of devices are connected.

Another way that data can get out of an organization's control is through the use of online data storage sites. These sites enable the transfer of data from a computer to a location accessible over the Internet. Most of these sites provide 5 GB or more of free storage. While it is certainly possible to blacklist these sites, there are so many, and more are being developed regularly, that it is difficult, if not impossible, to block access to them all.

#### 5- Train Employees: Develop a Culture of Safety

One of the greatest security assets of a business is its own employees, but only if they are properly trained to comply with security policies and detect potential security issues.

Many employees do not understand the importance of various security policies and practices. As mentioned before, they view these policies as nothing more than an inconvenience. Gaining employee support and commitment takes time, but it's time well spent. Start by carefully explaining the reasons behind any security implementation. One reason might be to ensure employee productivity, but focus on security issues first. Downloading and installing unapproved software can install malicious software that can infect user systems and cause their computers to run slowly or not work at all.

Perhaps the most direct way to gain employee support is to let employees know that the money needed to respond to attacks and fix user-initiated issues is money that cannot be used for raises and promotions. Letting employees know that they now have a “stake in the game” is one way to involve them in security efforts. If a budget is allocated to respond to security issues and employees help keep the budget within budget, the difference between the money spent and the actual budget can be split among employees as a bonus. Just as employees will be more likely to speak up when they notice network or system slowdowns, they will also likely be more likely to confront strangers wandering around the facility.

The purpose of these activities is to encourage employees to approach management or the security team voluntarily. Once this starts happening regularly, you will expand the capabilities of your security team and create a much more secure organization.

#### 6- Defining and Using Built-in Security Features of Operating System and Applications

Many organizations and system administrators state that they cannot create a secure organization because they have limited resources and do not have the funds to purchase robust security tools. This is a ridiculous approach to security because all operating systems and many applications include security mechanisms that require no corporate resources other than time to define and configure these tools. A great resource for Microsoft Windows operating systems is the Microsoft Learn platform. [https://learn.microsoft.com/tr-tr/security/](https://learn.microsoft.com/tr-tr/) You can access the guide created for Windows Server 2022 from the link: <https://info.microsoft.com/rs/157-GQE-382/images/EN-WBNR-eBook-SRDEM134534ebook.pdf>

One of the biggest concerns in an organization today is data leakage, the ways in which confidential information can leave an organization despite robust perimeter security.It's the impressions. As mentioned before, USB Flash drives are a cause of data leaks; Another is the recovery of data located in unallocated clusters of a computer's hard disk. Unallocated clusters, or free space as they are commonly known, are hard disk space where the operating system and applications discard leftovers or leftover data. Although this data cannot be viewed through a user interface, it can be easily identified (and sometimes recovered) using a hex editor such as WinHex.

![](/images/1_DFq_JJiwXTd39CS0mUtrUQ.png)

WinHex displaying the contents of a deleted Word document

If a computer is stolen or donated, it is very possible for someone to access data located in unallocated clusters. For this reason, many people have difficulty finding a suitable “disk cleanup” utility. Many such commercial tools exist, but there is one built into Microsoft Windows operating systems. The command line program cipher.exe is designed to view or change the encryption of directories (files) stored on NTFS partitions. Very few people know this command; Even fewer are familiar with the /w switch. Here's a description of the key from the program's Help file:

"Removes data from available unused disk space on the entire volume. If this option is selected, all other options are ignored. The specified directory can be anywhere on a local volume. If it is a mount point or points to a directory on another volume, data on that volume is removed."

To use Cipher, click Start Run and type cmd. When the cmd.exe window opens, type cipher /w:folder; where folder is any folder on the volume you want to clear and then press Enter. Figure 1.8 shows Cipher deleting a folder.

Another source of data leaks is personal and regulatory information that can be associated with Microsoft Office files. In Microsoft Word, you can configure the application to remove personal information on save and alert you when you are about to print, share, or send a document with tracked changes or comments.

To access this feature, in Word click Tools Options and then the Security tab. At the bottom of the security window you will see the two options described previously. Just select the options you want to use.

Implementing a strong security posture often starts with making the login process more robust. This includes increasing the complexity of the login password. All passwords can be cracked given enough time and resources, but the harder you make a password to crack, the more likely it is that the asset the password protects will continue to be protected.

All operating systems have some mechanism for increasing the complexity of passwords. In Microsoft Windows, this can be accomplished by clicking Start, Control Panel, Administrative Tools, Local Security Policy. Under Security Settings, expand Account Policies and then highlight Password Policy. You can enable password complexity in the right-hand panel. Once this is enabled, passwords must contain at least three of the following four password groups:

* Uppercase characters (A to Z)
* Lowercase characters (a to z)
* Numbers (0 to 9)
* Non-alphabetic characters (such as !, $, #, %)

It is important to know that all operating systems have embedded tools to assist with security. Finding these often requires some research, but the time spent identifying them is less than the money spent purchasing additional security products or recovering from a security breach.

#### 7- Monitoring Systems

Even with the most robust security tools in place, it is important to monitor your systems. All security products are man-made and can malfunction or be compromised. As with other aspects of technology, one should never rely on a single product or tool. Enabling logging on your systems is one way to put your organization in a position to identify problem areas. The question is, which log should be recorded? There are some security standards that can help determine this. One of these standards is the Payment Card Industry Data Security Standard (PCI-DSS). Requirement 10 of PCI DSS requires organizations to “Network resourcesThis requirement is an excellent approach for a log management program. If you substitute confidential information for cardholder data, this requirement is an excellent approach for a log management program. Requirement 10 is reproduced here:

> Logging mechanisms and the ability to track user activities are critical. Having logs in all environments enables comprehensive monitoring and analysis in case something goes wrong. Without system activity logs it is very difficult to determine the cause of a hazard.

1. Create a process to bind all access to system components (especially access with administrative privileges, such as root) to each user.
2. Implement automatic audit trails for all system components to reconstruct the following events:  
   - All individual user access to cardholder data  
   - All actions performed by anyone with root or administrative privileges  
   - Access to all audit trails  
   - Invalid logical access attempts  
   - Use of identification and authentication mechanisms  
   - Initialization of audit logs  
   - Creation and deletion of system level objects
3. Record at least the following audit trail entries for all system components for each event:  
   - User identification  
   - Event type  
   - Date and time  
   - Success or failure indicator  
   - Start of the event  
   - Identity or name of the affected data, system component or resource
4. Synchronize all critical system clocks and times.
5. Secure logs from tampering:  
   - Limit viewing of logs to those with business-related needs.  
   - Protect log files from unauthorized changes.  
   - Back up log files immediately to a central log server or hard-to-modify media.  
   - For wireless networks, copy logs to a log server on the internal LAN.  
   - Use file integrity monitoring and change detection software on logs to ensure existing log data cannot be changed without generating alerts.
6. Review the logs for all system components to ensure minimum logs are kept. Log reviews should include servers that perform security functions, such as intrusion detection system (IDS) and authentication, authorization, and accounting protocol (AAA) servers (e.g., RADIUS).  
   Note: Log collection, parsing and alerting tools may be used to ensure compliance. (like SIEM)
7. Keep logs for at least one year.

Requirement 6 seems a bit challenging because few organizations have the time to manually review log files. Fortunately, there are tools that will collect and parse log files from various sources. All these tools have the ability to notify individuals of a particular event.

An even more granular approach to monitoring your systems is to install a packet capture tool on your network so you can analyze and capture traffic in real time. One tool that can be very useful is Wireshark, “an award-winning network protocol analyzer developed by an international team of networking experts.” Wireshark is based on the original packet capture tool Ethereal. Analyzing network traffic is not a trivial task and requires some training, but it is perhaps the most accurate way to determine what is happening on your network. #### 8- Hire a Third Party to Audit Security

No matter how skilled your staff is, there's always a chance they may have missed something or misconfigured a device or setting. That's why it's crucial to bring an extra set of "eyes, ears, and hands" to review your organization's security posture.

Although some IT professionals become paranoid about having a third party review their work, smart personnel will recognize that a security review by strangers can be a great learning opportunity. The advantage of having your systems inspected by a third party is that outsiders have experience examining a wide range of systems, applications, and devices across a variety of industries. Knowing what works well and what might work but cause problems in the futureare attachments. They are also more likely to know about new vulnerabilities and the latest product updates. Why? Because that's all they do. They are not hindered by administrative tasks, internal policies, and help desk requests. They will be more objective than in-house staff and will be in a position to make recommendations after their analysis.

Third-party analysis should include a two-pronged approach: They should determine how the network appears to attackers and how secure the system is should attackers breach perimeter defenses. External review, often referred to as penetration testing, can be performed in several ways; Firstly, it is an uninformed approach where consultants are given absolutely no information about the network and systems before their analysis. Although this is a very realistic approach, it can be time consuming and very expensive. Consultants using this approach should use publicly available information to begin enumerating the systems to test. This is a realistic approach, but partial information analysis is more efficient and less costly. If a network topology diagram and a list of registered IP addresses are provided, third-party reviewers can complete the review faster and results can be handled in a much more timely manner. Once the penetration test is completed, examination of the internal network can begin. An audit of the internal network will detect open shares, unpatched systems, open ports, weak passwords, rogue systems, and many other problems.

#### 10- Patch, Patch, Patch

Almost all operating systems have a mechanism that automatically checks for updates. This notification system must be open. While there is some debate about whether updates should be installed automatically, system administrators should at least be notified of updates. Since patches and updates are known to cause more problems than they solve, they may not want them to be installed automatically. An example of this is the crovdtrike case we experienced recently. <https://tr.euronews.com/next/2024/07/21/crowdstrikein-neden-oldugu-kesinti-dunya-capinda-85-milyon-bilgisayari-etkiledi>

However, administrators should not wait too long before installing updates, as this could unnecessarily expose systems to attack.

**Use Administrative Accounts for Administrative Tasks**A common vulnerability occurs when system administrators perform administrative or personal tasks while logged into their computers with administrative rights. Tasks such as checking emails, surfing the Internet, and testing suspicious software can expose the computer to malicious software. This means the malware can run with administrator privileges and create serious problems. Administrators should log in to their systems using a standard user account to prevent malware from taking control of their computers.

**Restrict Physical Access**With the focus on technology, it is often easy to overlook non-technical security mechanisms. If an intruder can gain physical access to a server or other infrastructure asset, the intruder will own the institution. Critical systems should be kept in secure areas. A secure area is one that provides the ability to control access only to those who need to access systems as part of their job responsibilities. A room that is kept locked with a key given only to the system administrator, with the sole copy kept in a safe in the office administrator's office, is a good start. The room should not have any windows that can be opened. Additionally, there should be no labels or signs on the room indicating that it is a server room or network operations center. Equipment should not be stored in a cabinet accessible to other employees, officials or contractors. The validity of your security mechanisms should be reviewed during a third-party vulnerability assessment.

**Don't Forget Paper!**With the development of advanced technology, people have forgotten how information was stolen on paper in the past. Managing paper documents is quite simple.Locked filing cabinets should be used and locked at all times. Extra copies of proprietary documents, document drafts, and expired internal communications are some of the materials that should be destroyed. A policy should be established that tells employees what they should and should not do with printed documents. The following example of the theft of trade secrets highlights the importance of protecting paper documents:

> A company security camera captured Coca-Cola employee Joya Williams going through files at her desk and “stuffing documents into bags,” Nahmias and FBI officials said. In June, an undercover FBI agent met another of the defendants at an Atlanta airport and gave him $30,000 in a yellow Cookie box in exchange for an Armani bag containing secret Coca-Cola documents and a sample of a product the company was developing, authorities said.

The mentioned steps to achieve security are just the beginning. They should give you an idea of ​​where to start creating a secure organization.
